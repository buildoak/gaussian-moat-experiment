The Gaussian Moat

A visual guide to the transfer matrix approach — number theory meets Anderson localization. For an engineer who builds things, not one who proves theorems.

Section 1

Your Primes — The Raw Picture

Gaussian primes live on a 2D lattice. Each dot is a number a+bi whose norm a²+b² is a rational prime. The question: can you walk from the origin to infinity, stepping only on primes, with bounded step size?

The question

For step size √2, the walk dies at norm ~26. For √4, around ~36. Nobody knows if ANY finite step size suffices to reach infinity. That is the Gaussian Moat Problem.

Section 2

The Cylinder — Strip Geometry

Far from the origin, the Gaussian lattice looks locally flat. We can unwrap an annular ring into a flat strip. But the key insight is fixed physical-width strips, not fixed-angle wedges. At large R, a fixed-angle wedge becomes enormous.

30

An annular ring at radius R, thickness δ=3. "Unroll" to watch it flatten into a strip. Notice: Δθ(R) = W/R keeps strip at constant physical width. At large R the curvature is O(1/R).

Your idea = cylinder coordinates

Your "translate primes to (0,0)" idea = local cylinder coordinates. GPT-5.4 Pro corrected the density normalization: shell thickness H(R) ~ ln(R), not R·ln(R). The density only falls from 0.035 to 0.028 between R=80M and R=10B — the local environment changes slowly. Fixed physical-width sampling via Δθ = W/R gives comparable local stats across all radii.

Section 3

Raindrops on a Wire — Hecke & Rudnick-Waxman

At radius R, Gaussian primes hit the circle at certain angles. Are those angles structured, or random? If random — no hidden rhythm can help or hurt the walk.

200

Primes with norm near R², projected onto the unit circle by angle. Increase R: the points always look uniformly scattered.

Actual prime gaps

Random (Poisson) gaps

The two histograms are nearly indistinguishable. That is the Rudnick-Waxman result (2019).

Why this matters

Hecke (1920) proved primes spread evenly on average. Rudnick-Waxman proved even the fine-grained gaps look random. No hidden rhythm. The prime walk is fighting pure randomness — which is exactly where Anderson localization theory applies.

Section 6

Signal Decay — Anderson Localization

A signal enters a random medium. It bounces, scatters, and its amplitude decays exponentially. The decay rate γ (the Lyapunov exponent) tells you everything.

50%

Top: wave propagating through a random medium, amplitude decaying. Bottom: log(amplitude) vs distance — straight line = exponential decay. The slope is the Lyapunov exponent γ.

γ < 0 — Signal grows

Robust transport. Many paths reinforce. The walk reaches far. No moat here.

γ = 0 — Critical

Balanced on the edge. Transport neither grows nor decays. The phase transition.

γ > 0 — Signal dies

Localization. Amplitude decays exponentially. A moat MUST exist at finite radius.

In 2D with random disorder — which is what Rudnick-Waxman says the primes look like — Abrahams, Anderson, Licciardello & Ramakrishnan (1979) proved that γ is ALWAYS positive. No escape. Transport always dies in 2D.

Measure the decay, find the moat

Your transfer matrix composition? The Lyapunov exponent is the rate at which the largest singular value of the composed matrix grows. Measure it at a few radii, extrapolate where transport dies — that is the moat. You do not need to find the moat. You measure the decay rate and predict it.

The economics
