The Gaussian Moat

A visual guide to the number theory and physics behind the transfer matrix approach. For an engineer who builds things, not one who proves theorems.

Section 1

Your Primes — The Raw Picture

Gaussian primes live on a 2D lattice. Each dot is a number a+bi whose norm a²+b² is a rational prime. The question: can you walk from the origin to infinity, stepping only on primes, with bounded step size?

√40

Gaussian primes up to |z| < 50. The circle shows which primes are reachable from the hovered prime in a single step of size ≤ √k. Can you walk to infinity?

The question

For step size √2, the walk dies at norm ~26. For √4, around ~36. Nobody knows if ANY finite step size suffices to reach infinity. That is the Gaussian Moat Problem.

Section 2

The Cylinder — Unwrapping the Annulus

Far from the origin, the Gaussian lattice looks locally flat. We can "unwrap" an annular ring into a flat strip — cylinder coordinates — and the curvature vanishes.

An annular ring at radius R, thickness δ=3. Hit "Unroll" to watch it flatten into a strip. Primes (dots) preserve their angular positions. At large R, the strip is effectively flat — curvature is O(1/R).

Your idea ↔ The math

Your "translate primes to (0,0)" idea — working in local coordinates centered on each tile — is exactly this. The math calls it conformal flattening or local cylinder coordinates. You got there by engineering instinct.

Section 3

Raindrops on a Wire — Hecke & Rudnick-Waxman

At radius R, Gaussian primes hit the circle at certain angles. Are those angles structured, or random? If random — no hidden rhythm can help or hurt the walk.

200

Primes with norm near R², projected onto the unit circle by their angle. Increase R and watch: the points always look uniformly scattered.

Actual prime gaps

Random (Poisson) gaps

The two histograms are nearly indistinguishable. That is the Rudnick-Waxman result (2019).

Why this matters

Hecke (1920) proved primes spread evenly on average. Rudnick-Waxman proved even the fine-grained gaps look random. No hidden rhythm. The prime walk is fighting pure randomness — which is exactly where Anderson localization theory applies.

Section 5

Resistor Network — Flow and Conductance

Treat primes as nodes, step-reachable pairs as edges (resistors). Connect a battery: inner boundary = +, outer boundary = −. Watch the current flow.

5

Current squeezes through bottlenecks. One narrow path = high resistance, fragile. Many parallel paths = low resistance, robust. Effective resistance grows as shells stack in series.

Your idea ↔ The math

Your "flow theory / electricity inspired" idea — this is exactly the Schur complement. Eliminate interior nodes, keep boundary response. The math calls it Kron reduction. You called it "the operator." Same object.

Section 6

Signal Decay — Anderson Localization & Lyapunov

A signal enters a random medium. It bounces, scatters, and its amplitude decays exponentially. The decay rate γ (the Lyapunov exponent) tells you everything.

50%

A signal propagating through a random medium (top) and its amplitude envelope (bottom). The exponential decay rate is the Lyapunov exponent γ.

γ < 0 — Signal grows

Robust transport. Many paths reinforce. The walk reaches far. No moat here.

γ = 0 — Critical

Balanced on the edge. Transport neither grows nor decays. The phase transition.

γ > 0 — Signal dies

Localization. Amplitude decays exponentially. A moat MUST exist at finite radius.

In 2D with random disorder — which is what Rudnick-Waxman says the primes look like — Abrahams, Anderson, Licciardello & Ramakrishnan (1979) proved that γ is ALWAYS positive. No escape. Transport always dies in 2D.

Your idea ↔ The math

Your transfer matrix composition? The Lyapunov exponent is the rate at which the largest singular value of the composed matrix grows. Measure it at a few radii, extrapolate where transport dies — that is the moat. You do not need to FIND the moat. You measure the DECAY RATE and predict it.
