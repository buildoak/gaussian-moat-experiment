## 2. Theoretical Bounds from Percolation Theory

### 2.1 Lower Bound from Density Arguments

The Gaussian prime density in the first octant behaves as:
$$\rho(N) \sim \frac{4}{\pi \ln N}$$

At distance r, the number of primes in a annulus of width w is approximately:
$$N_{primes} \sim \int_{r-w/2}^{r+w/2} \frac{4\pi r'}{\pi \ln r'} dr' = \frac{4}{\ln r} \int_{r-w/2}^{r+w/2} r' dr' \approx \frac{4rw}{\ln r}$$

For a connected path to exist, we need typical inter-prime distances ≤ √40 ≈ 6.32. The mean spacing s satisfies ρ·s² ≈ 1, so:
$$s \approx \sqrt{\frac{\ln r}{4r}}$$

Setting s = 6.32 gives:
$$40 \approx \frac{\ln r}{4r} \times 40$$
$$1 \approx \frac{\ln r}{r}$$
$$r \approx \ln r$$

Solving numerically: **r ≈ 1.5** — absurdly small. This naive mean-field argument fails because primes cluster into constellations ( Eisenstein and Gaussian prime patterns).

### 2.2 Upper Bound from the Known Bounds at k²=34,36

The upper bounds tighten dramatically:
- k² = 34: UB < 24,289,452 → log < 17.01
- k² = 36: UB < 80,015,782 → log < 17.20

These are *upper bounds* (the actual values could be much smaller), but they constrain the growth rate. If R_moat(36) were anywhere near 80 million, we'd expect R_moat(34) to be at least 20–30 million. The fact that the computational search couldn't find a path to infinity at k²=36 even with 80 million as a *bound* suggests the component terminates well before this.

The deceleration pattern strongly suggests **R_moat(40) < R_moat(36) × constant**, where the constant is < 2 based on the trend.

## 3. Mechanism of Moat Formation

### 3.1 Critical Assessment of Hypotheses

**Hypothesis A: Local Prime Depletion (Annular Gap)**
This is the classical picture: as you move outward, primes become sparser (density ~ 1/log r), and eventually the typical spacing exceeds the step size, creating an impassable ring.

**Hypothesis B: Origin-Lineage Extinction (Branching Process)**
This is the mechanism suggested by the "Codex audit" — the connected component containing the origin is a branching process. Even if primes are locally dense, the specific primes reachable from the origin may fail to branch sufficiently to keep up with the expanding frontier.

### 3.2 Evidence for Branching Process Dominance

The key evidence is the **component size data**. At k²=32, the component contains 103,711,268,594 primes — roughly 75% of all primes in the first octant up to that distance — yet it still terminates. This is not a case of global depletion; the vast majority of primes exist but are *not connected to the origin's component*.

This strongly supports Hypothesis B: the moat forms not because primes are absent, but because **the specific primes reachable from the origin form a sparse subgraph** that eventually fails to propagate. It's a percolation "local extinction" event rather than a density threshold crossing.

### 3.3 Implication for Extrapolation

If moats form via branching process extinction, the growth of R_moat is governed by the **critical exponent of directed percolation** on the Gaussian integer lattice. The deceleration we observe (diminishing increments in log R) is consistent with approaching a critical threshold where the percolation probability drops sharply.

This suggests R_moat(40) may be **closer to the k²=36 upper bound than the linear extrapolation predicts**, because the branching process is approaching its extinction threshold.
