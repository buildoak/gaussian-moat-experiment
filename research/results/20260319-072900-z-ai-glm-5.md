## 1. Mechanism of Moat Formation

Before predicting numbers, I need to address the fundamental mechanism question.

**My conclusion**: Moats form via **connected component fragmentation**, not local prime depletion.

### Evidence for Fragmentation Mechanism

1. **Component size ratios**: At k²=32, the component contains ~75% of all generated primes in the first octant. If moats formed via density gaps, we'd expect this ratio to approach 100% near the moat. Instead, the component "dies" while primes remain locally abundant.

2. **Percolation theory precedent**: In 2D continuum percolation, finite components can be arbitrarily large even above the critical density. The origin's component is one such finite component—it dies due to branching process extinction, not because it reaches a "desert."

3. **Prime density at predicted distances**: At R ≈ 300M, the Gaussian prime density is ρ ~ 4/(π ln R) ≈ 0.06 per unit area. With coordination number p(40) = 18, the expected degree is ~18 × 0.06 ≈ 1.1. This is barely above critical (degree ~1), suggesting the component dies when the branching process fluctuates below replacement—exactly the fragmentation mechanism.

### Why This Matters for Prediction

If moats formed via density gaps, we could predict R_moat by finding where local density falls below a threshold. But with fragmentation, the process is stochastic—the component can die at various distances depending on the specific prime configuration. This introduces irreducible variance into any prediction.

## 5. Why the Deceleration Occurs

The growth rate deceleration has a mathematical explanation:

**As p(k) increases, the marginal benefit of additional edges diminishes.**

In percolation terms, the critical density scales as ρ_c ~ 1/z where z is the coordination number. But the actual density at distance R is ρ(R) ~ 1/ln R.

Setting these equal: 1/ln R ~ 1/z, so ln R ~ z.

This gives **linear** scaling: log(R) ~ p(k).

But there's a correction: not all edges are equally useful. As p(k) grows, edges connect to more distant primes, which are:
1. Less likely to exist (lower density)
2. More likely to connect to already-visited regions (redundancy)

This creates a **sublinear correction**: log(R) ~ p(k)^β with β < 1.

Fitting to the data suggests β ≈ 0.85-0.95, explaining the deceleration.
