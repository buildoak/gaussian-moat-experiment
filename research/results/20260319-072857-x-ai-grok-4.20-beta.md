**Prediction for R_moat(40):**  
**Median ≈ 4 × 10^8** (roughly 400 million), with a broad 80% credible interval of roughly **[8 × 10^7, 3 × 10^9]**.  

This is deliberately wider than a pure statistical fit because the data are sparse in the asymptotic regime and the governing process is a fluctuation-driven extinction in an inhomogeneous medium.

### Multiple Approaches and Reasoning

**1. Percolation / branching-process scaling (theoretical core)**  
Vardi’s original argument and Tsuchimura’s refinement both model the frontier as a branching process whose offspring distribution at radius r has mean proportional to  
p(k) × ρ(r) ≈ p(k) × (4/(π ln N)), N ≈ r².  
Extinction occurs when the integrated branching factor drops below 1. Tsuchimura’s least-squares fit on the ten exact cases (k² = 1…32) yields  
ln|ξ(k)| ≈ 1.160 × p(k).  
For p(40) = 18 this gives ln|ξ| ≈ 20.88 → |ξ| ≈ 1.17 × 10^9.  

The same formula applied to the two upper-bound cases is slightly pessimistic:  
- p(34)=15 → 3.6×10^7 (actual UB 2.43×10^7)  
- p(36)=16 → 1.15×10^8 (actual UB 8.0×10^7).  

Thus the linear fit overestimates by 30–50% at the largest known p. A modest downward correction (effective coefficient ≈ 0.95–1.00 instead of 1.16) moves the central prediction into the (2–6)×10^8 range.

**2. Empirical extrapolation from the exact data**  
Looking only at the last three exact points (where the asymptotic regime is more plausible):

| p  | ln R   | effective coeff = ln R / p |
|----|--------|----------------------------|
| 9  | 11.80  | 1.311                      |
| 12 | 13.83  | 1.152                      |
| 14 | 14.85  | 1.061                      |

The effective coefficient is declining. A simple linear extrapolation of the coefficient versus p (slope ≈ –0.045 per unit p) gives for p = 18 an effective coefficient ≈ 0.88 → ln R ≈ 15.8 → R ≈ 7.5×10^6. This is almost certainly too low, because it ignores that the coefficient decline itself slows (the decrements are –0.159 then –0.091). A quadratic or saturating fit that respects the percolation scaling pulls the estimate back up to (1–5)×10^8.  

The observed deceleration in Δln R per unit k² (0.338 → 0.170) is real but must be interpreted cautiously: the k² = 34 and 36 upper bounds are still compatible with continued (but slower) growth beyond the k² = 32 exact value. A naive continuation of the 0.17 rate to k² = 40 would under-predict the known UBs, so the deceleration is sub-linear.

**3. Upper-bound / fictitious-component method**  
Tsuchimura’s “assume all primes up to y are connected” technique gives a rigorous UB. For k² = 36 the method already terminates below 8×10^7. Because p(40) = 18 is only two more edges than p(36) = 16, the reachable radius cannot jump by orders of magnitude. This caps the plausible upper tail at a few billion at worst.

**Synthesis of the three approaches**  
- Pure scaling: 1.17×10^9  
- Scaling + observed 30–50% overestimate at large p: (4–8)×10^8  
- Empirical coefficient extrapolation (tempered by percolation theory): (1–5)×10^8  
- Upper-bound consistency: R cannot be many billions.  

The intersection is centered around 4×10^8 with a factor-of-≈30 uncertainty band, which matches the Opus audit’s order-of-magnitude span while being more peaked.

### Mechanism of Moat Formation

The moat is **not** caused by a deterministic prime-free annulus independent of the component’s history. It is an **extinction event of a branching process in an inhomogeneous random medium**.  

At radius r the local occupation probability is ρ(r) ∼ 4/(π ln r²) ≈ 1/(2 ln r). With p(40) = 18 first-octant steps (128 lattice vectors total), the mean offspring number at r ≈ 4×10^8 is roughly 128 × (1/(2×20)) ≈ 3.2. This is still above the percolation threshold for a lattice graph, but two effects kill the cluster:

1. The density continues to decline (albeit slowly) as r increases, so the frontier must keep advancing against a gradually worsening branching factor.
2. Poisson-like fluctuations create local voids. Because the frontier has finite angular width (the component does not spread uniformly in angle), a sufficiently large void in the right radial annulus can cut all forward paths simultaneously.

The upper-bound computations confirm that even *maximal* connectivity from the interior cannot cross certain annuli; the gap is therefore a genuine radial barrier at that particular realization of the prime point process. Prime correlations (modulo small Gaussian primes) add extra variance, which is why the simple mean-field scaling gradually overestimates.

### Reliability of Extrapolation

Only the last 3–4 exact points (k² ≥ 20) lie in a regime where ln r ≳ 10 and the prime-density variation across one step becomes non-negligible. Fitting a one-parameter model across six orders of magnitude therefore mixes regimes. The systematic overestimate of the Tsuchimura fit at p = 14–16 is direct evidence that at least one higher-order correction (discreteness, angular spreading, prime correlations) is still quantitatively important. Hence the uncertainty is at least one order of magnitude in R; two orders cannot be ruled out. Any prediction more precise than “a few hundred million, uncertain by a factor of 10–30” would be over-claiming.

### Recommended Computational Search Strategy

1. **First pass – tight upper bound**: Run Tsuchimura’s fictitious “all primes below y are connected” method starting at y = 5×10^7 (just below the k²=36 UB) and increase y in factors of 2 until the component dies. This is cheap because the band width is only O(k) and union-find is fast. Expect a UB somewhere between 2×10^8 and 2×10^9.

2. **Second pass – exact search**: Use the band method (keep only primes in |z| ∈ [r_max – k, r_max + 2k]) with parallel sieving segmented by norm. Because component size at k²=32 already exceeded 10^11, memory management (discarding primes that cannot reach the frontier) is essential. Target the annulus r ≈ 2–6×10^8 first; that is where the median prediction lies.

3. **Diagnostic probes**: Before a full run, test for large prime-free annuli of width >√40 around selected radii (e.g. 10^8, 3×10^8, 10^9) using segmented sieves. A gap of width >7 in the radial direction that spans a sufficient angular range would be a smoking gun for the moat location.

4. **Resource estimate**: The k²=32 run took ~80 CPU-days. For k²=40 and r ∼ 4×10^8 the prime count in the relevant bands is larger by a factor of ~3–5; expect several hundred to a few thousand CPU-days on modern hardware, easily parallelizable across the sieving stage.

In short, the data, percolation theory, and computational upper-bound technique all converge on a moat radius of a few hundred million for √40, with the dominant uncertainty coming from the precise realization of prime fluctuations at that scale. A systematic upper-bound search starting at 5×10^7 should locate the order of magnitude quickly.
