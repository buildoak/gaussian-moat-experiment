### 1. The Mechanism of Moat Formation: Annular Gap vs. Lineage Extinction
There are two ways a connected component can terminate:
1. **Origin-Lineage Extinction (Branching Process):** At small $k^2$, the origin's component is just a lucky, tenuous path that eventually dies out due to local fluctuations, long before the global density of primes forces it to.
2. **Local Prime Depletion (Annular Gap):** At large $k^2$, the component becomes a macroscopic "infinite cluster." It absorbs almost all available primes. It only terminates when the global prime density $\rho(R)$ drops below the critical percolation threshold $p_c(k)$, creating a literal "moat" (an annular gap) that no path can cross.

**Evidence from the data:** 
For $k^2 = 32$, the component size is $103.7$ billion. The total number of Gaussian primes in the first octant up to $R = 2,823,054$ is approximately $\frac{R^2}{4 \ln R} \approx 134$ billion. The component contains **~76% of all primes** in that region. This is a massive, dense cluster. It is not dying from a random branching fluctuation; it is dying because it hit a hard physical limit—the critical density threshold. 

Therefore, for $k^2 = 40$, the moat will form via **local prime depletion**. The termination distance is deterministically governed by the radius where the prime density drops to the percolation threshold.
