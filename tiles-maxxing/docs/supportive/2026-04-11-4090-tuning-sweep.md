## Phase 3: mont_to_gpu Fix

Removed `a % ctx.m` from `mont_to_gpu()`. Both MR witnesses are always < m (witness 1 = 2, witness 2 = uint16_t from FJ64 table), so the modulo is redundant.

**Result:** Within measurement noise. Clean A/B comparison:
- Baseline: 142,751 / 142,543 / 143,160 tiles/s (median ~142,800)
- With fix: 137,884 / 138,089 / 137,438 tiles/s (median ~137,900)

The "fix" was actually slightly slower in back-to-back testing. On the 4090's massive ALU throughput, the single uint64 modulo is completely hidden by OoO execution. **Mont_to_gpu fix: negligible on 4090.**

## Phase 4b: K2 Register Sweep

| K2 maxreg | Actual Regs | Blocks/SM | K2 Time (ms) | tiles/s |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|--------------|---------|
| uncapped | 46 | 4 (75.0%) | ~8.3 | 133,174 |
| 48 | 46 | 4 (75.0%) | 8.324 | 132,263 |
| 46 | 44 | 4 (75.0%) | 7.962 | 135,221 |
| **44** | **44** | **4 (75.0%)** | **7.984** | **135,785** |
| 42 | 42 | 4 (75.0%) | 8.027 | 134,987 |
| 40 | 40 | 5 (93.8%) | 8.238 | 132,965 |

**Winner: maxreg=44** — same as Jetson. Forces nvcc from natural 46 to 44 regs, improving instruction schedule without spills. At 40 regs: 5 blocks/SM (93.8% occupancy) but the extra block can't compensate for the degraded instruction mix.

Key finding: the 40→44 boundary behaves identically on sm_89 as on sm_87. 288 threads x 44 regs = 12,672 regs/block. At 65,536 regs/SM: floor(65536/12672) = 5 blocks but 5 x 12672 = 63,360 which overflows the actual allocation (5 x 288 x 44 = 63,360 < 65,536). Wait — actually at 44 regs the allocator gives 4 blocks (75%), meaning it rounds up per-thread allocation to maintain alignment. The occupancy calculator confirms 4 blocks/SM at 44 regs.

## Phase 4c: L2 Cache Persistence (__ldg)

Added `__ldg()` to FJ64 table read (512 KB in global memory, fits easily in 4090's 72 MB L2).

**Result:** 135,483 tiles/s — no measurable difference. The `__restrict__` pointer qualifier already enables the compiler to use LDG.LD.CONSTANT on sm_89. The table is guaranteed L2-resident at 512 KB / 72 MB = 0.7% of L2.

## Phase 4e: Block Size Comparison

| Threads/Block | K2 Blocks/SM | K2 Occupancy | tiles/s (2000) | Correctness |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| 256 | 5 | 83.3% | 172,691 | **BROKEN** (primes=0) |
| **288** | **4** | **75.0%** | **~144K** | **Correct** |
| 320 | 4 | 83.3% | ~143K | Correct |

256 threads breaks correctness — kernels assume 288 threads for row coverage (257 columns). 320 threads works correctly but performs identically to 288 at 2000 tiles. Stick with 288.

## What Matters, What Doesn't

**Matters:**
- **SM count and clocks** — the 2x scaling is almost entirely from hardware (128 vs 82 SMs, higher boost clocks)
- **Register cap at 44** — consistent win across both sm_87 and sm_89, ~2% over uncapped
- **Batch size** — larger batches amortize launch overhead, ~155K at 10K tiles vs ~144K at 2K tiles

**Doesn't matter:**
- **mont_to_gpu modulo removal** — within noise on 4090 (ALU-rich architecture hides the single division)
- **__ldg for FJ64** — 4090's 72MB L2 already caches the 512KB table, compiler uses LDG via __restrict__
- **320 vs 288 threads** — wash, correctness constraints keep us at 288

## Next Steps

1. **Multi-batch pipelining**: Launch multiple batches with CUDA streams to overlap K5 of batch N with K1 of batch N+1. The pipeline is strictly sequential within a batch but batches are independent.
2. **Persistent kernel K2**: Convert K2 to a persistent kernel that processes multiple tiles per block, reducing launch overhead at small batch sizes.
3. **A100 test**: HBM2e bandwidth may help K4 (UF has scatter/gather pattern). A100 also has 40MB L2 (plenty for FJ64).
4. **Production batch size**: At 155K tiles/s and ~10B tiles to process, total compute time would be ~18 hours on a single 4090.
